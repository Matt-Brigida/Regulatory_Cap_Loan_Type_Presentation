Literature

  • @dell2017bank found evidence that risk-taking by banks is decreasing in short-term interest rates, and this relationship is more pronounced for banks with high capital levels.
  • @schwert2018bank found firms dependent on banks for financing tend to borrow from well-capitalized banks, and firms with access to the bond market borrow from banks with less capital.
  • @noss2016estimating found that during period of economic growth, an increase in bank capital requirements is related to a reduction in lending.
  • @Baker_2015 showed how higher bank capital requirements can increase bank cost of capital through the low-risk anomaly.

Research on capital requirements is particularly timely given proposed regulation which would set a required tier 1 leverage ratio of 9% for bank with less than $10 billion in assets (Section 201 of the Economic Growth Regulatory Relief and Consumer Protection Act of 2018).

Goal of This Analysis

In this analysis we:

  • confirm the link between levels of regulatory capital, and the quantity of bank credit.
  • determining if capital levels have any effect on the type of bank credit offered.
  • determine whether risk weightings affect the types of loans bank offer.

Two Capital Ratios

  • Tier 1 Leverage Ratio: Tier 1 Capital divided by Total Assets
  • Tier 1 Risk-Based Capital Ratio: Tier 1 Capital divided by Risk-Weighted Assets
  • @haldane_2011 contrasted the number of calculations it takes a large bank to calculate its regulatory capital ratio under Basel I (a few) and Basel II (approximately 200 million).

Method

Results

Because capital ratios are nonlinearly related to bank health---both very low and very high capital ratios indicate poor financial performance---we also conduct our analysis on subsets of well and poorly capitalized banks.
